Why these areas?

FlipHaus covers 22 Stockholm areas: Farsta, Kista, Bagarmossen, Skarpnäck, Johanneshov, Bromma, Alvik, Stora Mossen, Enskede, Solna, Årsta, Hägersten, Hökarängen, Kärrtorp, Högdalen, Lilla Essingen, Stora Essingen, Östermalm, Södermalm, Finntorp, Ektorp and Sickla. They aren't picked at random — every one clears the same three filters that make a renovation flip work:

1. On the tunnelbana. Each sits on a metro line (not just commuter rail), so the commute to T-Centralen is short and predictable. Good transit keeps demand — and therefore resale liquidity — high.
2. Renovation-age housing stock. The stock spans the 1930s–50s folkhem and garden-suburb era, the 1960s–70s miljonprogrammet, and 1980s blocks (Skarpnäck) — but it is all now old enough that original kitchens, bathrooms and flooring are entering a major renovation cycle.
3. A measurable price gap. Each trades below inner-city prices while renovated apartments sell at a clear premium over unrenovated ones — the margin a flip captures.

We deliberately left some areas out: pendeltåg-only suburbs such as Sollentuna (weaker resale liquidity than the metro), already-gentrified or thin-margin areas like Blackeberg (where the renovated-vs-unrenovated gap is already priced in), and northern Sundbyberg (Rissne / Hallonbergen) — rental-heavy miljonprogrammet with too thin an owner-occupier resale market for a flip to exit reliably.

We also weigh resale liquidity — how many apartments actually sell in an area each year. A flip only works if you can sell soon after finishing, so thin markets (where a renovated unit can sit, racking up carrying cost) carry real exit risk. The badge on each area card below shows trailing-12-month sold volume.

Target areas

Farsta

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: 1956-1965 (early miljonprogrammet / folkhem era)

Transit: Farsta strand tunnelbana (Green line) — 18 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: Farsta was Sweden's first planned suburb and is now undergoing its biggest transformation since the 1960s. Stockholm stad is investing heavily in the Farsta centrum masterplan — new housing, retail, and public spaces. The existing apartment stock features classic 1960s layouts with dated wet rooms and kitchens ripe for modernisation. Prices per square metre remain among the lowest within the tunnelbana network, creating a wide margin for renovation uplift.

Kista

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: 1970s (miljonprogrammet)

Transit: Kista tunnelbana (Blue line) — ~20 min to T-Centralen

The setup: Kista is Stockholm's tech cluster ("Kista Science City"), built around Ericsson and largely original 1970s apartments — historically a deep, employed buyer base that paid a premium for renovated homes.

⚠️ Watch closely — prices falling on a structural shift, not just the rate cycle. Ericsson is relocating its Stockholm HQ out of Kista to Hagastaden and cutting ~1,600 Swedish jobs, and Q1-2026 office vacancy hit ~26.7% (double central Stockholm). The anchor employer that made Kista a flip area is leaving, so the recent fall isn't guaranteed to mean-revert just because rates have stabilised. Treat as comps/market data and hold off sourcing here until the trend below flattens and turns — that's the buy signal, not "it can't fall further".

Bagarmossen

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: 1950s-1960s

Transit: Bagarmossen tunnelbana (Green line) — ~14 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: A fast metro ride from the city with a strong neighbourhood identity and growing popularity among families and creatives priced out of Söder. Plenty of mid-century apartments with original interiors, and a clear, widening gap between renovated and unrenovated sale prices.

Skarpnäck

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: 1980s (terminus development)

Transit: Skarpnäck tunnelbana (Green line, terminus) — ~15 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: The green-line terminus, so trains start here and the commute is reliable. A mix of 1980s stock with dated finishes and steady demand from young families. Renovations modernising kitchens and bathrooms capture an obvious uplift against the area's lower entry prices.

Johanneshov

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: 1930s-1960s

Transit: Gullmarsplan / Globen tunnelbana (Green line) — ~10 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: Right on the edge of Södermalm around the Slakthusområdet redevelopment (one of Stockholm's largest urban projects). Very short commute, rapidly improving amenities, and older apartment stock that hasn't yet caught up to inner-city prices — a strong setup for renovation-led gains.

Bromma

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: Mixed, 1940s-1970s

Transit: Abrahamsberg and nearby green-line stations — ~20 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: An established, leafy west-side district that already commands strong renovated prices, so a well-executed renovation in an older BRF building converts directly into resale value. The opportunity is the dated apartments within otherwise desirable blocks.

Alvik

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: Mostly 1930s-40s funkis + later

Transit: Alvik (green line + Tvärbanan) — ~8 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: A close-in, waterside Bromma node with very strong, liquid demand. Prices already sit high, so the renovated premium is tighter than outer Bromma — but a dated apartment in a desirable 1930s-40s block still converts well. Best for confident, clean renovations rather than deep-discount plays.

Stora Mossen

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: Mainly 1930s-40s funkis/smalhus

Transit: Stora Mossen (green line) — ~10 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: A quiet, sought-after residential pocket one stop from Alvik with the same close-in Bromma demand. Older funkis apartments with original kitchens/bathrooms renovate into a clear premium; margins are tighter than the outer areas, so it rewards a tidy execution.

Why it's high potential: A sought-after south-side area with a garden-suburb character and a quick metro link. Demand is high and consistent, so modernised apartments sell well — and there's still a healthy stock of dated interiors to upgrade.

Solna

Municipality: Solna stad

Built: Mixed, 1950s-1970s

Transit: Solna centrum tunnelbana (Blue line) — ~12 min to T-Centralen

Why it's high potential: One of Stockholm's fastest-developing municipalities — Mall of Scandinavia, the Karolinska/Hagastaden life-science district and Friends Arena are all driving demand. Older apartments here renovate into a market with strong, rising prices and excellent transit.

Hägersten

Municipality: Stockholms kommun

Built: Mostly 1940s-50s folkhem + some newer pockets

Transit: Red line southwest — Hägerstensåsen, Telefonplan, Midsommarkransen (~9-15 min to T-Centralen)

What is the miljonprogrammet?

Between 1965 and 1974, the Swedish government built over one million homes in a single decade — the most ambitious public housing program in Western history. The goal was to eliminate the post-war housing shortage and give every Swede a modern home.

The result was vast suburban estates of standardised concrete apartment blocks, built quickly and efficiently. The construction quality was solid, but the interiors — kitchens, bathrooms, flooring, plumbing — were designed for a 30-year lifespan.

That lifespan expired 20-30 years ago. While some buildings have been renovated by their housing associations (BRF), a large proportion still have original interiors. This creates a massive, predictable renovation opportunity:

Original bathroom = linoleum floors, dated tiles, old fixtures
Original kitchen = white appliances, metallic benchtops, no dishwasher
Original flooring = worn parquet or vinyl throughout

FlipHaus uses AI to identify these indicators from listing photos, then estimates renovation cost based on room sizes — giving investors a data-driven view of which properties have the most upside.

Stockholm housing timeline

1930s-1950s

Folkhem era — Sweden builds its welfare state. Early planned suburbs like Farsta and Vällingby are created as self-contained "ABC cities" (Arbete, Bostad, Centrum).

1965-1974

Miljonprogrammet — One million homes built in a decade. Rissne, Tensta, Rinkeby, and hundreds of other suburbs are constructed. Standardised designs prioritise speed and cost.

1990s-2000s

First renovation wave — Plumbing and facades updated in many buildings, but interior renovations left to individual apartment owners. Many skip it due to cost.

2010s

Inner city prices surge — Stockholm's population grows rapidly. Young professionals begin looking outward. Suburbs with good transit see renewed interest.

2020s

The renovation gap — Unrenovated miljonprogrammet apartments trade at significant discounts to renovated ones. AI and data make it possible to identify and quantify this gap at scale. FlipHaus enters here.
